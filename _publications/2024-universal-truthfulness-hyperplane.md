### Abstract

Recent years have witnessed remarkable progress in large language models (LLMs). However, a fundamental challenge persists: models often generate content that deviates from factual reality or their internal knowledge, resulting in hallucinations that can have serious consequences in high-stakes applications. In this work, we discover and investigate a universal truthfulness hyperplane embedded within LLMs---a geometric structure that separates factual from hallucinatory representations in the model's internal space.

Through extensive experiments across multiple model families and scales, we demonstrate that this hyperplane is remarkably consistent and can be used to predict and mitigate hallucinations. Our findings provide new insights into the internal mechanisms governing model truthfulness and suggest practical approaches for building more reliable language models.
